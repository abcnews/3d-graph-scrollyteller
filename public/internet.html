Water network - for testing vis

Four Corners

Posted July 08, 2019 11:40:54

Australia's most important river system is in crisis, and recriminations are flying.

Everyone agrees there's not enough water. Scientists say there's not enough flowing to the environment. Irrigators say there's not enough going to crops.

At the heart of the fight is a plan: the most expensive environmental plan in Australia's history.

Passed into law in 2012, the $13 billion Murray Darling Plan tries to be win-win for both irrigators and the environment. At its heart is a simple idea: pay irrigators to use less water.

In a major compromise to win over irrigators, the plan no longer involves simple buy-backs of water, and now focuses on spending the money on "efficiency" measures.

As a result, billions of dollars are now being spent subsidising irrigation infrastructure on farms in the basin. The aim is to pay farmers to reduce wasted water. Some of the saved water is left in the river to help the environment and some of it is kept by the farmers.

In theory, everyone wins.

But Four Corners revealed this week the system isn't working.

The government isn't checking to see if water savings are real, and in some cases the infrastructure grants are enabling irrigators to pump extra water.

Professor Richard Kingsford, a river ecologist at the University of New South Wales says nobody is sure if the taxpayer money is actually saving water.

"There's poor transparency in the counting. It means that we're still not sure that we're getting the amount of water that we think back into the river to restore it to health."

Richard Beasley SC was the counsel assisting the recent South Australian royal commission into the Murray Darling Basin Plan.

"It still hasn't been disclosed to the public, what is the good, best available science behind this in terms of how much water we're getting out of these schemes," says Mr Beasley.

But on the other side of the ledger, parts of the irrigation industry are making a killing. Billions are being spent on infrastructure for farms, which means they can farm more intensively, expand their farms onto previously unviable lands, and often save on energy costs.

How did the biggest environmental plan in Australia's history become so skewed in favour of those exploiting the environment?

According to former Murray Darling Basin Authority director Marryanne Slattery, the big players in the irrigation industry has incredible access to the halls of power.

"It's perfectly reasonable for any constituent or any advocacy group to seek the best outcomes for themselves or the people they're representing - that's a perfectly normal and appropriate part of good democracy," says Ms Slattery, now a researcher at the progressive think tank, The Australia Institute.

"But you need government agencies to safeguard against it. They need to do the right thing. So that industry groups and individuals can't gain at the expense of others and create perverse policy outcomes, which is clearly what has happened."

"To me it's a failure of government," says Ms Slattery.

Critics say Big Water have the ear of government and have perverted the Murray Darling Basin Plan.

So the ABC has examined some of the connections that link government to big players in the irrigation industry.

Pouring over hundreds of social media profiles, ASIC documents and ASX announcements, what we can show you is just an exemplar of the sort of access the irrigation industry has to government.

Let's start with two of the key water infrastructure programs, through which a combined $1.5 billion has been paid to irrigators.

One company that Four Corners revealed had benefited significantly from them was Webster Limited - a foriegn-backed agricultural company, which grows 90% of Australia's walnuts as well as a lot of cotton.

It got tens of millions of dollars intended to put more water into rivers, and used that in part to build new dams, which will allow them to take more water from the rivers. The way the program has been set up, there is nothing wrong with that.

Webster owns more water in the Murray Darling Basin than any other irrigator, [Confirm this is still true after Tandou and other sales.] with rights to take up to 153 billion litres of water out of the Murray Darling Basin every year - a portfolio of entitlements it values at about $350 million dollars.

Much of the water Webster receives, is delivered via an irrigation network run by a company called Murrumbidgee Irrigation.

Murrumbidgee Irrigation also received massive grants through another similar federal government program.

They were handed $347 million dollars of taxpayer money intended to return water to rivers, to upgrade and expand their irrigation network, which supplies Webster and many other irrigators in the area. Those upgrades allowed more water to be delivered to irrigators faster. Again, under the policy in place, there's nothing wrong with that.

Murrumbidgee Irrigation Ltd owns the water network used by thousands of farmers in the Murrumbidgee: the shared channels, pipelines, pumps and storage dams that deliver water for irrigation and other purposes.

Murrumbidgee Limited is a corporation, owned by its customers.

The biggest customer - and biggest owner - is Webster Limited. [Is this 100% known? If not, pull back to "a major customer and owner".]

Webster benefited enormously from government policy that uses environmental money to modernise farms.

Webster's chairman is Chris Corrigan.

Mr Corrigan rose to fame in the 1998 waterfront dispute, when his company Patricks sacked its entire workforce with the support of then-Prime Minister John Howard and workplace relations minister Peter Reith.

The company hired balaclava-clad security guards and dogs to expel unionised employees, and bussed-in non-union workers. The successful union-busting won Mr Corrigan hero status among Australia's political right.

Corrigan has also been a significant donor to the Liberal party, with a company he led called Qube donating $51,000 to the Liberal party in 2012.

Mick Keelty was the chief of the Australian Federal Police until 2009. He's now been appointed as the Northern Basin Commissioner, and is investigating political donations and what influence they might have had on policy decisions in the Murray Darling Basin.

He says cases like that where donations are easy to find are rare. "It's very difficult to trace whether the lobby groups and others are influencing political parties either through donations," he says.

Mr Keelty says he's enlisted the help of researchers to trace donations between irrigators and politics, but he hasn't made much progress yet.

He says there's no problem with donations being made - but the lack of transparency is damaging.

"That creates a higher level of mistrust from other sectors."

But where donations might be hard to trace, the presence of irrigation lobby groups can't be missed.

"There is an absolute lobby industry in this in this space," he says.

"There's a large number of lobby groups that are permanently based in Canberra and whose role it is to lobby government on behalf of their constituents or their members."

Again, Mr Keelty's main concern is not with the lobby groups being there - but rather the lack of transparency over what access they have. "We don't know what their overall influence is on government decisions nor do we know what they donate how they donate to political parties."

Let's start by looking at just one, the NSW Irrigators' Council. And again using Webster as an example, we can see how these lobby groups could provide a conduit to influence government policy.

It is perhaps the most powerful in the Murray Darling Basin and a web of companies linked to Webster has deep ties to it.

Webster's General Manager in charge of water is Brendan Barry. Mr Barry is a board director of the NSW Irrigators' Council.

General manager in charge of policy at Murrumbidgee Irrigation, Karen Hitchinson, was a board director of the NSW Irrigators' Council until 2018.

In 2014 and 2015, Webster teamed up with the powerful Robinson family, primarily led by David and Joe Robinson, who owned a company called Austrailan Food and Fibre.

With that partnership, Webster quadrupled in size and became the biggest private water holder in the Murray Darling Basin. But it also gained a myriad of connections to lobby groups, held by the Robinsons.

During the partnership, Webster went through a series of acquisitions and sell-offs that meant at one time or another the entanglement included all of these companies.

But in addition, the Robinsons are major players in the irrigation lobby.

Joe Robinson himself - a former director of Webster who played a key role in some of its most significant deals - is chair of two local irrigator lobby groups and his father, David Robinson, is a former director of Cotton Australia.

The very point of these groups is to lobby for the best outcomes for their constituents. And the irrigator lobby - like many other lobby groups - has a revolving door keping its connection to politics close.

Lobbyists have come and gone from some of the key water policy-setting roles in politics.

Take the case of the NSW Irrigator Council's current CEO, Luke Simpkins. He's a former federal Liberal MP.

But flow doesn't just go one way - this is Perin Davey.

She was a director of the NSW Irrigators Council in 2015 and 2016. Her connections through industry and politics are substantial. She has been an advisor to former Nationals leader Ron Boswell. She was hand-picked by then-nationals leader and water minister Barnaby Joyce to be a director of the Murray Darling Basin Authority - but she withdrew after she was revealed as one of the lobbyists in on a secretly-taped phone call aired on Four Corners in 2017, in which she and others were offered sensitive information by the top NSW water bureaucrat.

Current Nationals leader, Michael McCormack, says he's worked with Davey for many years. She is the chair of one of the federal government's Regional Development Australia boards and is well connected through the National Party.

And this year she was elected as a federal senator for the National Party in NSW.

The National Irrigators Council also has deep connections to both the NSW and Federal governments.

Focus on NIC.

It's headed by Steve Whan. He was a Labor minister in NSW, and for some of that time had carriage over water as Minister for Primary Industries.

A former CEO of the council was Tom Chesson, a long-time advisor to a string of Nationals MPs, who was appointed by Barnaby Joyce to head up the National Carp Control Plan.

In 2019, Tom Chesson became an advisor to Melinda Pavey, the new NSW minister for water.

So we know that through these connections the owners of the companies in question have multiple ways of accessing both state and federal politicians.

But large irrigation corporations also keep close ties with the quiet but important world of the bureaucracy, from where policy advice is crucial.

Take Kaye Dalton. She's a director of Murrumbidgee Irrigation and an alternate delegate to the NSW Irrigators' Council.

Kaye Dalton has deep connections to the key bureaucratic decision-makers, politicians, and industry.

She previously worked in the NSW government running water buy-back programs, and then became the director of a program called Water for Rivers, jointly run by the federal, Victorian and NSW governments. It was a precursor to existing water buy-back schemes.

While at Water for Rivers, Kaye Dalton worked with director of the program Neil O'Keefe, who was previously a senior MP in the Keating Labor government.

In 21 March 2011, O'Keefe was part of a group that established a new company called Irrigation Efficiency Partners.

Within ten days of registering the company, it had successfully applied to receive nearly $16 million in on-farm efficiency infrastructure grants. And it went on to receive more than $60 million from the program.

Later that year, O'Keefe was sacked from the government's Water for Rivers program, after an ombudsman concluded a company chaired by O'Keefe had inappropriately received advanced knowledge about government plans that would benefit specific irrigators, allowing his company to target specific properties to purchase.

Kaye Dalton left Water for Rivers the same year, and focussed on her consulting company, The Risorsa Group (previously called Kaye Dalton Consulting).

Kaye Dalton, through The Risorsa Group, has since run the on-farm efficiency projects for O'Keefe's Irrigation Efficiency Partners.

Through those connections, Kaye Dalton's private company has administered the distribution of tens of millions of dollars of government subsidies to irrigators - including to Webster.

Commissioner Keelty says there's nothing wrong with people leaving government buraucracies, and gaining employment in the area they are expert in. "We can't deny people getting employment," he says.

"What we do [need] in those circumstances though is a higher level of transparency so everybody knows what the rules are, and everybody knows who are the gainers and who are the losers when decisions are being made," says Keelty.

And amongst all these groups, they sometimes come together in unofficial groupings, that are much harder to trace.

In 2017, Four Corners revealed one such grouping led by the top water bureaucrat in NSW, Gavin Hanlon. He held a teleconference - one an independent investigation later revealed was one of many - where he gave special access to a select group of irrigators and irrigator lobbyists.

One of the people on that call - Perin Davey - was a lobbyist who would soon herself enter federal parliament.

** find a segue to the closing quotes**

David Papps was in charge of environmental water held by the Federal Government until 2018. He says the lack of transparency in how decisions are made, and what influence is being exerted by irrigators is a major problem with the plan.

"There has been a lack of transparency. There has been, I think, a failure on the part of Basin states and the Commonwealth, to properly implement the plan, so the level of confidence we as a community can have, that there's value for money… We can't be confident, and, therefore, it's entirely possible, under the current state of arrangements, that perhaps big corporate irrigators have benefited more than they should have."

"Those concerns need to be addressed at the highest levels by government," he says.

Professor Richard Kingsford agrees. When it comes to what water is being saved with taxpayer money, he says nobody knows for sure.

"We haven't closed loop holes, our compliance is bad. There's poor transparency in the counting. It means that we're still not sure that we're getting the amount of water that we think back into the river to restore it to health," says Professor Kingsford.

"I'm really concerned I guess about the way In which um, money and water mix particularly in terms of power and influence."

And for Northern Basin Commissioner Mick Keelty, this lack of transparency is the most fundamental problem. "I mean you've got some big lobby groups and [if] these lobby groups are influencing government then we all need to know. They might be influencing in a very positive way and they might be influencing in a way that delivers a better outcome for the economy and a better outcome for employment. But I tell you what: I don't see it."
